MOVING AVERAGE CONVERGENCE DIVERGENCE

Written by Alain Waldispuehl - Friday, 04 February 2022

The Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD) is a strong trend-following momentum analysing tool. It calculates the movement of two Moving Averages and provides signals by an additional Moving Average, called the signal line. The MACD can also be used in many different ways.

The MACD is calculated by subtracting a slow period Moving Average from a faster period Moving Average. In my settings I use a 10-period EMA as a fast Moving Average and a 20-period EMA as a slow Moving Average.

The MACD would result of the following: 10-period EMA - 20-period EMA

The middle line or 0-line shows the distance between the two Moving Averages, this means if the MACD is above the middle line or greater than 0 then the fast Moving Average is above the slow Moving Average and vice versa.

The signal line is an additional Moving Average which is attached to the MACD. Here I use a 10-period EMA. The signals are provided whenever the MACD crosses over or under the signal line.

Further the MACD provides a Histogram which shows the momentum between the MACD and its Moving Average. The Histogram is positive or greater than 0 when the MACD is above its Moving Average. The Histogram is negative or less than 0 when the MACD is below its Moving Average. With the Histogram you can observe rising or falling momentum between the MACD and the Moving Average, both on the positive and on the negative side.
